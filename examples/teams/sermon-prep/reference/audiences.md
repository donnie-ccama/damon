# The Three Audiences (shared source of truth)

Every audience brief the Editor writes is a re-pitch of the **same** exegetical
findings — the same central claim, the same road to Christ — aimed at a different
reader. Nothing may contradict `10-exegetical-study.md`. What changes is the
vocabulary, the assumed knowledge, the kind of authority that persuades, and the
proof each reader needs before they'll grant a point.

This file is the definition of record. The Editor's skill enforces it; the
Orchestrator's QA checks against it.

---

## 1. The Theologian

**Who:** Biblically literate and *actively curious* about Scripture's teachings.
The most scholarly reader in the set. Reads the canon fluently, knows the
storyline, and wants the exegetical machinery, not a simplified takeaway.

**What they already grant:** the authority of the text. You do not argue *that*
Scripture speaks; you argue *what* it says and *how well* the reading holds.

**Pitch the brief so it:**
- Keeps the technical apparatus — original-language terms (with transliteration),
  discourse structure, typology, genre, canonical context.
- Engages the **cruxes** honestly: names the live interpretive options with their
  advocates and adjudicates, the way the study does.
- Enters the **scholarly dialogue** — where the reading sits against major
  alternatives, and why.
- Assumes the Christological road can be *named and defended*, not just felt.

**Voice:** rigorous, peer-level, unhurried. Confident but not glib. This reader is
insulted by hand-holding and delighted by a well-defended crux.

**Length target:** the fullest of the three (roughly 1200–1800 words).

---

## 2. The Practitioner

**Who:** *Not* Biblically literate, but stands to benefit from the values the
study uncovers in Scripture. Well-disposed toward those values; simply
unfamiliar with the text, the vocabulary, and the moves.

**What they already grant:** that Scripture's values are worth living. You do not
have to defend the goodness of the virtue; you have to make it **reachable and
livable**.

**Pitch the brief so it:**
- Translates every exegetical finding into **lived wisdom** — what this truth
  asks of an ordinary week.
- Keeps **one** anchoring passage reference, quoted plainly and explained; never a
  chain of citations or untranslated terms.
- Foregrounds the **value / virtue** the study surfaced and shows, concretely,
  what practicing it looks like — and what its counterfeit looks like.
- Names the honest cost of living it, so the encouragement survives contact with a
  hard week.

**Voice:** warm, plain, formation-oriented. A trusted mentor, not a lecturer. No
insider jargon; when a biblical idea is load-bearing, explain it in one clean
sentence.

**Length target:** roughly 900–1300 words.

---

## 3. The Skeptic

**Who:** Young, secular, and skeptical — **not** necessarily antagonistic. They
will follow a point *as long as it is grounded in sound logic and carried by clear
illustration.* Limited vocabulary (write to an **8th-grade** level). They do
**not** recognize the authority of Biblical teaching at face value.

**What they already grant:** nothing on authority. A claim earns its place by
**reason, lived experience, and a concrete illustration** — never by "because
Scripture says so."

**Pitch the brief so it:**
- Argues from **logic and example first**. Every claim is earned on the ground
  before any source is named.
- Uses **plain, short sentences** and everyday words. No religious jargon, no
  insider references, no assumed metaphysics. If a term needs a seminary to parse,
  it does not appear.
- Leads with a **clear illustration** the reader already knows from their own
  life, then draws the point out of it.
- Treats the reader as an **honest equal**, never a target. Names the strongest
  objection out loud and answers it fairly.
- May reference the passage as *the source of an idea worth testing* — never as a
  proof that settles the matter.

**Hard bans for this brief:** no preaching, no altar call, no "the Bible clearly
teaches," no untranslated Greek/Hebrew, no assumed belief in God as a premise, no
vocabulary above 8th grade, no shaming.

**Voice:** calm, plain, respectful, curious. The tone of someone reasoning *with*
you at a table, not *at* you from a stage.

**Length target:** the leanest of the three (roughly 700–1100 words).

---

## The through-line (all three)

- **One truth, three doors.** The central exegetical claim and the road to Christ
  are identical across the briefs. Only the door changes.
- **Never exceed the study.** No brief may assert what `10-exegetical-study.md`
  does not support. If the study flagged something as uncertain, the briefs keep
  it uncertain.
- **No moralism anywhere.** "Be a better person" is not the point of any brief.
  The Practitioner and Skeptic briefs still travel a real road; they just travel
  it in the reader's own language.
